I did this exercise to help myself justify switching jobs from one that had a 25-mile one-way commute attached to it to one that I could ride my bike to.

Having a drive commute was one of the main reasons I decided to buy a new car in 2015. I went on a walk with a friend today and told him that I wish I would have kept my old, trusty, 300k mile LS400 because it would have been a much better finincial decision. About a year ago, he was considering buying a $32,000 Audi A4 right after his roommate bought a new Subaru. Even after you're out of school, you can still become a victim of peer pressure.

When I did this exercise two years ago, I assumed that I would make the exact same amount at my new job, but would not be spending money on commuting or workday lunches, a typical perk at most small software companies in San Francisco.

A little better

Now, after being at my job at the small enterprise software company for a year and a half, I think it's high time to do the exercise again, especially since I'm pretty fed up with my situation once again, so I feel that a change is in order. I think it's always a good idea to establish a "business-as-usual" baseline before making a change, so you can make sure that after the change, you will actually come out better off.

But it doesn't have to just be about money, and the criteria is subjective. You may be at a point where you want to get more time, so you cut your hours or switch to a less-demanding job.

Before we do that, let's take a quick look at the methodology I used two years ago to figure out how much more I would actually take home just from deleting my car commute.

The best business is one that makes a profit

Think of yourself as a business. You are the CEO, middle management, and the individual contributor.

The CEO in you decides where you want be and sets the vision for your future.

The middle management in you takes that vision, and decides, without doing it, what work needs to be done to get there.

The individual contributor goes out and does all of the bitchwork that needs to be done to get you there.

There are costs associated with the work that you need to do as an individual contributor.
